Our case

Our study investigates the diachronic shift in the usage of the two Japanese quasi-synonyms (jisatsu, to commit suicide) and (jigai, to commit suicide, to hurt oneself).

(jisatsu) (To take ones own life; an act of ending ones life by ones own volition.) (By extension, refers to any self-negating or despair-driven act.)

(jigai) (To inflict harm on oneselfi.e., to end ones own lifeespecially including ritual or formal suicide such as a samurais seppuku.) (Used in contexts indicating an act of self-sacrifice to achieve an objective or to show strong resolve.)

Representative examples In Natsume Ssekis Meiji-period short story we find:

(He could no longer bear the suffering of his illness and committed suicide.)

(The samurai carried out suicide in order to preserve his honor.)

In a Shwa-era novella by Akutagawa Rynosuke:

(That young man attempted suicide in the depths of despair,)

(and the brave warrior on the battlefield proudly carried out suicide.)

Explanation of term choice In Japanese, there is also (seppuku)the ritual disembowelment historically performed by samuraias a counterpart to suicide. However, we chose (jigai) for our comparison with (jisatsu) for several reasons:

Broader semantic scope

(seppuku) refers very specifically to the formal samurai practice of self-disembowelment, bound by historical and class-based ritual contexts.

(jigai), by contrast, appears not only in descriptions of ritual suicide but also in a wider range of literary and historical contextscovering any self-inflicted death carried out with a sense of solemnity or duty, beyond strictly samurai circles.

Diachronic prevalence

In pre-Meiji and Meiji-period texts, is the dominant literary term for formal or honor-driven self-harm, whereas is reserved almost exclusively for narratives involving samurai.

From Taisho onward, continues to surface in modern fiction and non-fiction whenever an author wishes to evoke a formal or dignified self-sacrifice, while remains tied to historical or military settings.

Quasi-synonymic contrast

Because our goal is to trace the gradual shift from any formalized self-harm () to the more clinical or general term , provides the best paired counterpart: it shares the core sense of self-caused death yet occupies a distinct register and pragmatic function.

Thus, by comparing and , we capture the evolution from a broad literary usage of formalized self-harm to the modern, neutral designation of suicidesomething that alone could not illustrate.

process_text <- function(text, model) {
  anno <- udpipe_annotate(model, x = text)
  anno_df <- as.data.frame(anno)
  tokens <- anno_df$lemma[anno_df$upos %in% c("NOUN", "VERB")]
  paste(tokens, collapse = " ")
}

df$cleaned <- sapply(df$context, process_text, model = model)

Model

Since we have a large number of textual predictors (words in context) and a binary target variable (Formal vs.General), we apply binary logistic regression. This model allows us to evaluate how the presence of specific words affects the probability of a context being classified as Formal rather than General.

Results

As a result, we have received confirmation of our hypothesis, however, the database for an ideal statistical analysis is not balanced enough, but for existing occurrences we have observed some significant correlation.

Limitations of the Analysis

  • The corpus is not representative of all historical Japanese texts, especially given the dominance of Meiji-era data.
  • Some authors (e.g., Akutagawa, Mori gai) are heavily overrepresented, while others appear once or twice.

Future plans

  • Include data from other sources to have more era and author variability
  • Change the approach to data preprocessing, since grammatical indicators (such as particles, auxiliary verbs, or common connectors) can obscure the influence of meaningful content words
